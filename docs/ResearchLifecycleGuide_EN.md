# Part 1 — A Researcher's First Steps

**Lessons Learned**
- The tasks *around* research shape into 6 phases — the order is the strategy (license → articulate → show → survey → target → write)
- ORCID = your researcher's license. Write your question & hypothesis on 1–2 A4 pages and rewrite regularly — "mental strength training"
- References follow the 70% post-2020 rule — literature-researcher surveys, literature-reviewer verifies. Use short communications and the arXiv custom strategically
- research-starter accompanies every phase as a 23-slot state machine

## 1.1 The 6 phases — do them in this order!

| # | Phase | Key point |
|---|---|---|
| 01 | ORCID registration | Get your researcher's license — "LinkedIn for researchers". Set up the Scholar family too |
| 02 | Articulate question & hypothesis | Answer What / Why / How on 1–2 A4 pages. Choose by 3 axes: love × can × sells |
| 03 | LP, card & branding | LP, pre-registration form, card and an MVP: become a magnet as "the person doing X" |
| 04 | References & prior work | Map the top researchers and build your "playbook". The 70% post-2020 rule |
| 05 | Venues & planning | List 3 journals (with IF) + 3 conferences. Draft a one-year submission plan |
| 06 | Paper-writing skills | Start IMRAD and English writing now. Compete on creativity × outcome |

⚡ **The order is the strategy**: license (01) → articulate (02) → show (03) → survey (04) → target (05) → write (06).

## 1.2 Phase 01: ORCID registration — get your researcher's license first

**What is ORCID?** Open Researcher and Contributor ID. A persistent international identifier that links your papers, projects and affiliations — a researcher's LinkedIn / CV. e.g. `0000-0002-1825-0097`. Register now: https://orcid.org

**Why do it first?**
- It is your license to do research — without it you barely exist
- Major publishers (Nature, Elsevier, …) manage authors by ORCID
- Increasingly a required field at submission
- Citations, co-authorships and outputs get tracked automatically

Also set up Google Scholar / ResearchGate / Semantic Scholar. Automatic citation counts and h-index; link them to ORCID for one-stop management. Always complete your profile: affiliation, research topics, education.

**Drill**: [ ] register at orcid.org and record URL & iD  [ ] complete your profile  [ ] link the Scholar family

## 1.3 Phase 02: Articulate your question & hypothesis — A4 × 1–2 pages becomes Chapter 1

| Question | Content | How to write |
|---|---|---|
| **W** What? | Which phenomenon or problem interests you? | Write plainly: "X fascinates me", "Y is puzzling" |
| **Y** Why? | Why does it matter? Who is struggling? | Name the societal / natural / global theme clearly |
| **H** How? | How might it be solved? (hypothesis) | State it as "perhaps …" using a DS approach |

Update it any time. Rewriting regularly is the point — strength training for how you think.

**3 axes for choosing a topic**: what you love (pure curiosity — can you sustain the passion?) / what you can do (does it leverage your strengths?) / what you can sell (does it connect to real needs?).

In data science, the societal / global / natural theme you choose is itself what matters. DS techniques are the "how" — the value of the theme is the value of the research.

## 1.4 Phase 03: LP, business card & branding — show your research, become a magnet

- **Landing page**: one page for your topic, the problem you solve and the value you offer. "Coming Soon" is fine before launch. Webflow, Notion, etc.
- **Pre-registration form**: deployable today even with nothing released. Counts of interested users (and their attributes) become quantitative evidence of need. Google Forms works.
- **Business card**: name, affiliation, topic, LP URL and a QR code. Express yourself in one line as "the person doing X". Canva works.
- **Web app (MVP)**: minimal features are enough — productize the hypothesis. Qualitative feedback and usage tracking become research data.

**Magnet strategy**: once "the person doing X" brand is established, information, introductions, collaborators and users are drawn to you. Pre-registration counts and usage logs later serve as quantitative evidence of need in your papers.

## 1.5 Phase 04: References & prior work — the playbook and citation styles

**The playbook — the 70% post-2020 rule**: reviewers judge paper quality from the *look* of your reference list.
- Recent work (**70%**): centered on 2020 onward
- Authorities: a few classic masterpieces
- Data sources: original sources can be old
- Know the top 5–10 researchers by face, name and affiliation; follow them on Google Scholar. Map where your work sits, and picture the **world-line**: how the world changes if this is solved

**Citation styles (AMA vs APA)**

| | AMA (numbered [1][2]) | APA (author-year (Smith, 2023)) |
|---|---|---|
| Strengths | Strategic: you control citation order / easy to cite the venue's latest papers early | Robust: numbers never shift / author names appear in the text |
| Cautions | Adding a reference shifts every number | Writing author names every time |
| Main fields | Medicine, life sciences, CS | Social sciences, psychology |

Match the target journal's style. If neither is mandated, **AMA is strategically advantageous**.

Division of labor: survey & playbook = **literature-researcher** / verification, summaries & URLs = **literature-reviewer**. Unverifiable sources are flagged.

## 1.6 Phase 05: Know your venues, plan your schedule — build a track record strategically

1. **Cite the target venue's latest issue early**: cite the journal's recent papers early in the intro — a silent signal that you follow their venue.
2. **Use short communications**: report results quickly in ~3,000 words. Ranked on par with full papers. Ideal as a first publication!
3. **Use supplementary material**: when references or data do not fit the body, link them as an appendix — modern academic etiquette.

**DS / AI community custom — arXiv**: posting preprints to arXiv is the industry standard for speed. Citing them is generally discouraged (not peer-reviewed), but in **AI / ML it is acceptable when explicitly labeled as a preprint**. arXiv posting usually runs in parallel with conference / journal submission — use it actively.

**Drill**: [ ] list 3 top journals (with IF) + 3 top conferences  [ ] draft a one-year submission plan (deadlines, venues, topics)

## 1.7 Phase 06: Build writing skills + research-starter

**Phase 06**: international venues assume English — start building English paper-writing skills today. Professional expression: compete on creativity × outcome. Drill: [ ] draft title & 200-char abstract  [ ] outline each IMRAD chapter  [ ] make the figure/table that carries your claim.

**research-starter — a 6-phase state machine**
- Starts from conversation alone, no template needed. Inspects 23 slots and interviews you with gap flags + fulfillment questions
- Asks only about the current phase, and proposes exactly one next action (the order, mechanized)
- Generates & updates the ResearchStart DOCX (history: done / updated question / next action)
- Naming: `{YYYYMMDD}_ResearchStart_@{NAME}_{LANG}_vX.docx`. When all phases pass, hand over to paper_template_v0 → paper-compiler.

# Part 2 — Project Start: Design the Project

**Lessons Learned**
- Write the plan with a fixed outline: put "who does what, by when" (schedule & roles) right after the overview — task confirmation first
- Prevent salami slicing: state each deliverable's independent contribution and the cross-citation relationships
- Write the FAQ in words a junior-high student understands; pre-empt methodological criticism
- research-proposer converges to a "kickoff-ready" version via the gap-flag / fulfillment-question loop; literature-reviewer appends related work at the end

## 2.1 The research proposal — fixed outline & writing requirements

**Fixed outline (strictly in this order; section headings carry no numbers)**

- Researchers and affiliations (ORCID / e-Rad)
- **Schedule overview (by date)** ★ at the top: every milestone (deadlines, notifications, camera-ready, presentations, go/no-go) in ascending date order, each row carrying a **task list** (one bullet per line, marked ✅ done / ⚠️ in progress / ❌ not started)
- Overview (abstract, one-line core claim, keywords)
- Background (problem / limits of existing methods / novelty)
- Objectives, research questions, core contributions
- Experiment plan and methods (IDs, metrics, owners)
- **Schedule and roles** ★ a master table merging publication strategy, portfolio, deliverables and author assignment (with a "Deadline" column, **sorted earliest first**)
- **Venues, materials, links** (venue selection and fit; also sorted by deadline)
- Literature Review — generated by literature-reviewer
- FAQ (in words a junior-high student understands)
- **Update history** ★ at the very end, preceded by a page break; deleted in the kickoff release

**Task lists in the schedule overview**: show what remains before each milestone as bullets marked `✅ done / ⚠️ in progress (owner: open point) / ❌ not started` — **one task per line**. The markers mean the same as the slot-matrix verdicts, so a review can flag any milestone whose deadline is near while ❌ items remain as a HIGH-priority issue.

**The master table (Schedule and roles)**: `Deadline | Deliverable | Venue | Type | Authors | Distinct contribution | Rationale | Owner` in one table, sorted by deadline (TBD last). Alongside it: role split, anti-salami rationale, preprint policy, dependencies and the nearest go/no-go — with dates identical to the schedule overview at the top.

**Key points**
- **"Who, what, when" comes first**: highlight go/no-go decision points; state deadlines, notifications and camera-ready dates up front; note dependencies.
- **Prevent salami slicing**: if splitting into multiple papers, show each contribution's independence and cross-citations; map deliverables to speed / authority / permanence.
- **Pre-empt the critics**: define terms in the FAQ; prepare answers to methodological criticism for reviewers, collaborators and general readers.

## 2.2 research-proposer — compile the plan in dialogue

Manages the fixed outline as a 22-slot state machine. Starts from conversation, notes and minutes alone — no template required (successor to research-project-plan, extended into a dialogue loop).

**Loop**: material → mapping table (which info goes into which slot, shown up front) → gap detection (flags + fulfillment questions; flagging alone is forbidden) → answer & add (questions batched into one round) → DOCX update (4-item history entry, version +1).

- **Priority = kickoff necessity**: HIGH = researchers, overview, schedule, RQs / MEDIUM = background, portfolio, experiments, strategy / LOW = links, FAQ
- **Edits are Word-native**: rewrites = tracked changes, questions = comments anchored to the text. Inline annotations are forbidden.
- **Convergence = kickoff-ready**: 0 missing and 0 HIGH partials → delete the history page → distribute → open a paper_template_v0 per deliverable.
- Naming: `{YYYYMMDD}_{PROJECT}_Proposal_@{PI}_{LANG}_vX.docx`. Trigger: "research proposal", "draft the project plan".

# Part 3 — Choosing the Writing Format: IMRAD, 7 Sentences, Hako

**Lessons Learned**
- IMRAD is the world standard. The division of roles — especially separating facts (R) from interpretation (D) — earns credit for intellectual honesty
- Title & Abstract sit outside the format yet matter most. Design for the reviewer's reading order: Title, Abstract, figures, body
- Build the abstract and Chapter 1 as a "closed world" with the 7-sentence method; the hako template (Kiyoki lineage) reuses fixed phrasing verbatim
- Top international journals do not open with "We propose". Every number needs meaning: a baseline and stakes (Prof. Oka's feedback)

## 3.1 Why care about structure? The international standard: IMRAD

**Three benefits of learning structure**
1. Readers can predict what comes next, lowering their cognitive load
2. You notice what you have NOT written, reducing gaps in the paper
3. Riding the conventions professionals share tangibly raises acceptance odds

**IMRAD**: I (Introduction: set the question, position the work) → M (Methods: reproducible procedure; life sciences use "Materials and Methods") → R (Results: present the facts) → D (Discussion: interpretation, limitations, implications). The logic: **why ask → how investigated → what was found → what it means**.

Formally adopted by ANSI in 1972 and 1979. Since the 1980s virtually every scientific journal uses it. A novel result alone is not enough — reviewers must follow the argument in limited time. Scope: original research articles.

## 3.2 Chapter tips (1/3): Introduction and Literature Review

**I — the first gate: context and question**
- Include: field overview & significance / the gap (what is unknown) / RQ & hypothesis / explicit contributions
- Tips: the entrance that convinces readers it is worth reading. **Funnel from broad background to focus to this work**. The gap justifies the work's necessity.
- Example (3-step funnel): "X has spread rapidly in recent years (broad). However, Y remains poorly understood (gap). This study proposes Z, contributing … (this work)."

**L — draw the map of the debate**
- Include: comprehensive collection / grouping by issue / each work's contribution & limits / a bridge to the gap this work fills
- Tips: write **"what is known and unknown"**, not "who said what". In short conference papers, a Related Work section suffices.
- Example (closing bridge): "Thus, no method satisfies both X and Y simultaneously. This study fills that gap."

Slot mapping: I = blueprint I1–I5 (importance / gap / approach declaration / contribution list / roadmap); L = R1–R3 (by issue / two-axis positioning / delta vs. prior work). Checked by audit items 12–13.

## 3.3 Chapter tips (2/3): Methods and Results

**M — so a third party can reproduce it**
- Include: design / subjects, data, N & selection criteria / equipment, software & versions / analysis & statistics / ethics approval (human subjects)
- Tips: life sciences & agriculture title it **Materials and Methods**; social sciences & engineering usually just Methods. Objective, descriptive; no interpretation. Keep asking: could someone reproduce this?
- Example: "Analyses used Python 3.11 and scikit-learn 1.4. Participants were 32 (criteria: …). Approved by the … ethics board (No. …)."

**R — facts only, no interpretation**
- Include: observed / measured facts / figures & tables (the stars) / brief text pointing at their key points / negative results too
- Tips: "suggests" and "is considered" belong in Discussion. Always reference figure/table numbers in the text. Report numbers as they are.
- Example (bad → good): ❌ "accuracy is considered improved" → ✅ "the proposed method achieved 92.4% accuracy, exceeding the baseline (85.1%) (Table 2)."

Skeleton caveat: "no interpretation in Results" is an IMRAD rule (audit item 15). The hako template's Experiment blocks include verbal interpretation in step (6), so the rule does not apply there (N/A).

## 3.4 Chapter tips (3/3): Discussion and Conclusion

**D — the intellectual core that gives meaning**
- Include: one-paragraph summary of key results / comparison with prior work (agreements, disagreements, and why) / theoretical & practical implications / limitations / future directions
- Tips: do not repeat Results — speak about meaning. Not hiding limitations builds trust. **Answer "so what?"**. In CS & engineering, merging Results + Discussion is common.
- Example (compare → reason → limit): "This result supports hypothesis X. Unlike Smith et al. [3], we observed Y; a possible reason is Z. A limitation is that our subjects are restricted to W."

**Whether to add a Conclusion is a field convention**
- Empirical papers → separate Conclusion recommended (Discussion runs long; restate contributions briefly)
- Short conference papers → conclude inside Discussion (page limits: a closing paragraph suffices)
- Theses → separate Conclusion nearly mandatory (a chapter surveying contributions, limits and outlook)

Always check the venue's Author Guidelines and recent papers before submitting (audit items 16–17).

## 3.5 The extended form ILMRAD (5 chapters) — writing the Literature Review

A Literature Review chapter is inserted between Introduction and Methods. Widely used in full journal papers and theses. CS conferences usually call it Related Work.

- **Organize by issue (not chronology)**: not "the lineage of method A then B" — set up evaluation axes (issues). A year-by-year list is a failure mode.
- **Two-axis positioning**: set two axes and show "prior work does at most one; ours does both". Example: "real-time processing × personal adaptation — existing work does one or the other; ours does both". A figure helps.
- **Delta vs. your group's prior work**: name the foundational prior work explicitly and make this paper's delta crystal clear.

## 3.6 Title and Abstract — the crucial elements OUTSIDE the format

The acronym IMRAD excludes Title, Abstract and References — yet these are the paper's face, read first.

**Reading order**: ① Title (relevant?) → ② Abstract (worth reading?) → ③ Figures & Tables (what was found?) → ④ Full body (details?).

**Two-stage: Working then Final**
- Working Abstract (draft): written before you start, a provisional final form that serves as the paper's compass. It is expected to change.
- Final Abstract (for submission): rewritten last, after the body is done — yet read first by reviewers. Check that the Abstract + figures alone convey the skeleton.

**Structured Abstract**: the abstract itself structured as a miniature IMRAD; increasingly standardized. Background/Intro → Introduction / Objective → end of Introduction / Methods → Methods / Results → Results / Conclusions → Discussion.

## 3.7 References and Supplementary Material — modern academic etiquette

When to use supplementary material:
- **Supplementary references**: when the reference list overflows the body, link the rest as Supplementary References
- **Extended methods**: full protocols recorded to guarantee reproducibility
- **Extra figures & raw data**: supplementary figures, raw data and analysis code while keeping the body readable
- **Large data & video**: formats that cannot be embedded (CSV, video, spreadsheets, …)

**Reference quality check (audit items 08–11)**: [ ] overflow moved to Supplementary and referenced from the body  [ ] ~70% from 2020 onward (N/A for the hako skeleton)  [ ] arXiv labeled "preprint" (allowed in AI/ML)  [ ] citation style (AMA/APA) matches the venue

## 3.8 The 7-sentence abstract & Chapter 1 — build a closed world (devised by Assoc. Prof. Asako Uraki)

| Sentence | Content |
|---|---|
| 1 | In general, a human (a brain process / function) does it this way. If tackling something humans cannot do: define "this is hard for humans" |
| 2 | However, this has not yet been realized in any system |
| 3 | Therefore, realizing it is an important challenge in this field |
| 4 | We propose (the paper title) |
| 5 | The essential feature of this work is that it … (the feature realizing sentence 1's function) |
| 6 | Realizing this work makes … possible |
| 7 | In our experiments, we built … and verified effectiveness and feasibility |

**Why a "closed world"?**: pulling vast related work and context into the background inflates the world-view until its completeness can no longer be defined. **Close a complete world first with the opening four sentences** (human function → unrealized → key challenge → proposal), then explain the proposal cleanly inside it. Most people write sentences 5–7 well — the battle is the first four.

**Expanding to Chapter 1**: reorder slightly and grow each sentence into a few, adding concrete examples. The skeleton is the same. Built into paper-compiler v2 as the Abstract / Intro recipe for IMRAD, and checked by audit item 18.

## 3.9 7 sentences worked example — P620 "Time-axis Granularity Synthesizer"

1. In general, humans understand conversation, music and behavior by freely switching the time granularity — coarser or finer — depending on the situation.
2. However, this dynamic switching of time granularity has not yet been realized in any system.
3. Therefore, a mechanism that dynamically synthesizes and switches time granularity is an important challenge in time-series data analysis.
4. We propose the "Time-axis Granularity Synthesizer".
5. The essential feature of this work is that it synthesizes and switches multiple time-axis granularities as combinations of element functions, mirroring human granularity switching.
6. Realizing this work enables multi-granularity contextual analysis of a single time series.
7. In our experiments, we built a prototype on real data and verified the method's effectiveness and feasibility.

**Points**: sentence 1 opens with a human function, not technology / four sentences close the world — no need to survey all related work / sentence 5 echoes sentence 1's function (switching ↔ switching) / the method name is a capitalized proper noun, used consistently.

## 3.10 The 5-chapter hako template — typesetting with fixed phrases (Kiyoki lineage)

Empty "boxes" annotated only with what belongs where; no content. Used only for **Type A/B × EJC / IOS Press venues (Kiyoki lineage)**. Where papers vary, follow P620 (Time-axis Granularity Synthesizer).

| Chapter | Content |
|---|---|
| Abstract | 1 general proposition 2 problem definition 3 method as proper noun 4 essential features 5 contribution list 6 experiment preview |
| 1 Background | importance → self-citation block → problem logic chain 1–5 → proposal preview → two-axis positioning |
| 2 Proposal | [A] concept → N elements → main function / [B] step overview + core of N elements (A/B switch) |
| 3 Realization | each element as an identical block (a)–(g); after equations use the "X indicates …" pattern |
| 4 Experiments | declare feasibility as the evaluation axis; each Experiment has (1)–(6); Discussion has 4 fixed slots |
| 5 Conclusion | re-declaration → features & contributions restated → experiment recap → the classic global human-health landing |

**Typesetting rules (auto-checked by paper-composition-checker, H-X01–18)**: never alter the fixed phrases, signature verbs (propose / present / define / realize / switch / adapt / apply) or the standing self-citation list. The method name is character-identical across all chapters. Chapter 2's notation is implemented as-is in Chapter 3. Abstract-5 contributions = Chapter-5 contributions. Keep we/our and the romanization "Kansei". IMRAD rules (70% rule, no interpretation in Results) do not apply.

## 3.11 Hako fixed phrases — examples (gray = box, do not alter / < > = variable)

| Position | Example |
|---|---|
| Abstract 3: proposal | `we propose the <Method Name>, which <what it realizes>.` |
| Abstract 4: essential features | `The essential feature of <Method> is ... : 1) <...>, 2) <...>.` |
| Abstract 5: contribution list | `Our contributions are summarized as follows: 1) <...>, 2) <...>.` |
| Abstract 6: experiment preview | `In our experiment, we <...> to demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of our method in the field of <fields>.` |
| Ch.1 para 5: proposal preview | `In this paper, we present/propose <Method> ... The overall picture ... is illustrated in Figure 1.` |
| After equations | `X indicates <meaning of X>.` — keep the identical sentence pattern vertically |

**Keys to consistency (H-X checks)**: Abstract-5's contribution list equals Chapter 5's (items and order). The method name is character-identical in the title, abstract and chapters 1, 2 and 5. Signature verbs and we/our are carried through every chapter.

## 3.12 Why formats are "cool" — and how fields differ

1. **Respect readers' time**: a structure whose chapters are predictable is far easier to read — a statement of respect for the reader.
2. **Separate fact from interpretation**: no interpretation in Results, meaning argued in Discussion — such papers are judged intellectually honest.
3. **A shared international language**: the same structure is standard at every university worldwide. It buys trust before the content debate begins.

> "Only those who can keep the format may break it when it matters."

**Field tendencies** (always check Author Guidelines and recent papers): medicine / life sciences = strict IMRAD, Materials and Methods / psychology / social sciences = ILMRAD common, APA also recommends IMRAD / CS conferences = IMRD base, Results+Discussion sometimes merged / CS journals = leans ILMRAD / engineering = IMRD base / humanities = IMRAD generally not applied.

**Article types**: original research = this guide's scope (IMRAD / ILMRAD). Systematic reviews follow PRISMA, case reports CARE, editorials thematic structures (out of scope).

## 3.13 Top-journal lessons 1 (Prof. Souichi Oka, feedback 1–2) — do not open with "We propose"

❌ `"This paper proposes the 10 Senses Model, an epistemologically grounded evaluation framework..."` — effectively "We propose". Opening with self-introduction puts your **weakest sentence in the strongest position** (sentence 1).

✅ **Three alternative openings (the Muto / Oka class of writing)**

| Opening | Example |
|---|---|
| Framework as subject (declarative) | `The 10 Senses Model is a general-purpose evaluation framework...` |
| Problem / failure first | `Conventional AI evaluation metrics fail systematically when...` |
| Finding / number first | `A 17.1% discrepancy rate between subjective and objective...` |

**Three hooks that make people read on**: a surprising finding ("one in six AI recommendations was wrong") / an open question ("why do patients ignore AI even when it is right?") / a paradigm break ("is high accuracy the same as being right? No"). If you have novelty, claim it hard on page 1.

**Consistency with the 7 sentences and hako**: the proposal declaration (sentence 4 / hako Abstract-3 "we propose") belongs mid-abstract. Open with sentences 1–3 to pull readers in; for top journals, upgrade the declaration to the framework-as-subject form. References: overseas papers by Prof. Yoshiyasu Takefuji (Musashino Univ.) and Dr. Souichi Oka (NTT IOWN Integrated Innovation Center).

## 3.14 Top-journal lessons 2 (Prof. Oka, feedback 3–4) — give numbers meaning; write concrete-to-abstract

**FB3: always say whether a number is good or bad**
- ❌ "a 17.1% discrepancy rate (non-negligible)" — no baseline; readers cannot judge severity.
- ✅ Give meaning in three steps:
  1. **Conversion**: "roughly one session in six"
  2. **Analogy**: "a diagnostic system that errs for one patient in six is deemed unsafe even at 100% accuracy" — and state that existing accuracy metrics cannot detect this gap
  3. **Stakes**: "for athletes at VDOT 73–76 (Olympic / World-Championship level), a single erroneous overload prescription can wreck months of periodization and cost a once-in-four-years opportunity" — severity scales non-linearly with the reader's (user's) level

**FB4: the order of abstract and concrete (for EJC)**
- ❌ framework → motivation → finding (feels like reading a proposal's table of contents)
- ✅ **concrete finding → universal meaning → generalize with the framework** (the Nature / Science style)
- EJC's taste for the conceptual does NOT mean "abstract is fine". It means **"claim a clear contribution at a high level of abstraction"**. Place concrete examples (sports coaching) as reinforcement; put philosophical terms (the ancient Greek triad, etc.) after the finding. When abstraction runs long, add one concrete sentence.

**In compiler v2**: write mode demands the conversion / analogy / stakes trio for every number slot; audit flags uninterpreted numbers with a fulfillment question.

# Part 4 — Writing the Paper: paper-compiler v2

**Lessons Learned**
- Design along 3 axes (Type A–D × Phase P1–P4 × venue). The phase is a contract on permissible claims; overclaiming is flagged
- compiler v2 has three modes: write / review / audit. Convergence is mechanized via gap flags + fulfillment questions + yellow highlighting
- paper_template has 4 parts; part 2 (Literature Review) is generated by literature-reviewer with unverifiable sources flagged
- Edit suggestions are Word-native (comments + tracked changes, fixed author name) — inline annotations are forbidden

## 4.1 The 3 design axes — type × phase × venue

**Axis 1: proposal type** — changes the internal structure of Methods / the proposal chapter

| Type | Name | Internal structure |
|---|---|---|
| A | Architecture proposal | concept → N elements → main function |
| B | Algorithm | step pipeline → element defs → integration |
| C | System / artifact | requirements → design decisions → implementation → ops insights |
| D | Empirical study | RQ → design → analysis → ethics |

**Axis 2: research phase = the evaluation contract** — changes what you may claim (overclaims are flagged)

| Phase | Name | Evidence & claim ceiling |
|---|---|---|
| P1 | Position | pilot experiment → feasibility only; no effectiveness claims |
| P2 | Implementation | working system + qualitative eval; no statistical generalization |
| P3 | Proposal | quantitative & statistical eval + ablation → effectiveness OK |
| P4 | Domain application | mixed eval + expert involvement → real-world utility |

**Axis 3: venue**: domestic workshop → international conference → top conference / journal → **EJC / IOS Press (Kiyoki lineage)**. Phase × venue determines the required experiment set, metrics, sample sizes and ethics statements (handled by evaluation-planner).

**Every venue needs a stated rationale** (a required column in the initial question sheet Q4 and in the proposal's portfolio table). Consider: community fit / format fit / indexing & visibility / speed vs. permanence / the field's de facto standard.

**A caveat on IEEE/ACM-centrism**: do not rank venues by "IEEE/ACM = better". Depending on the nature of the contribution, a venue outside IEEE/ACM may be the right answer — say so explicitly in the rationale.
- **Theory / conceptual models** → IOS Press / EJC can be superior in both format and community
- **Software permanence** → SoftwareX / JOSS sit outside IEEE/ACM yet are the **de facto standard for software publication** (DOI, citability, peer-reviewed permanent archive)
- **Speed** → arXiv (citable in AI/ML when labeled a preprint)
- Conversely, in CV / NLP / HCI the visibility of a top conference is itself the value

Examples: "Deliverable 4 (theory) → IOS Press / EJC: superior in both format and community for a theoretical contribution." "Permanence for deliverable 2 → SoftwareX: outside IEEE/ACM, but the de facto standard for software publication; DOI-based citability matches the goal." Where the choice is contested, record both sides in the rationale and reflect it in the FAQ.

## 4.2 Skeleton selection rules and the A/B decision protocol

**Skeleton selection rules**
1. IMRAD is the default (paper-blueprint-designer)
2. The 5-chapter hako only for Type A/B × EJC / IOS Press (Kiyoki lineage) (paper-composer + composition-checker)
3. Types C & D always use IMRAD. Never mix skeletons

**A/B decision protocol (from the fill-in editor)**
- Centered on a system / concept model / components → A
- Centered on steps / a procedure / input–compute–output → B
- State the chosen type at the top of the output ("Adopted version: A")
- Ask exactly once, only if genuinely torn. Never add structure the boxes do not have

Even across skeletons, the slot matrix is the shared vocabulary. IMRAD = T → I → R → M (swapped by type) → E (rigor swapped by phase × venue) → D → C → REF. Hako = HA (Abstract 1–6) → H1–H5 → H-REF plus cross-chapter checks H-X01–18. Write generates, review inspects — two faces of one machine.

## 4.3 paper-compiler v2 — the write / review / audit modes

Absorbs the former paper-review-imrad: drafting, revision and pre-submission audit in one skill, auto-routed by input and trigger.

| Mode | Role | Trigger | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| write | Generation | material only / "turn this into a paper" | Maps material to slots, generates full-text draft + gap detection. Writes in the skeleton's house style (7 sentences / hako phrases) |
| review | Convergence loop | draft vX / "what is missing?" | Inspects via the slot matrix → gap flags + fulfillment questions → answers → recompile → version +1. Updates the review-history table |
| audit | One-shot gate | finished draft / "pre-submission check" | The 18-item pass/fail gate (paper-auditor). 3+ missing or a HIGH issue → proposes moving to review |

**references (unified -er agent names)**: paper-blueprint-designer (IMRAD blueprint + 7 sentences) / paper-composer (hako original) / paper-composition-checker (H-X consistency + A/B protocol) / paper-auditor (18 items) / evaluation-planner (type × phase × venue).

## 4.4 Running the write / review loop — kill the gaps and the paper is done

**Loop**: material (bullet points, minutes, transcripts, notes) → mapping table (item-to-slot mapping shown up front) → generate + detect (full text generated; gaps flagged with fulfillment questions) → answer & add (minimal writing effort) → recompile (version +1, 4-item history entry).

**Convergence**: 0 missing and 0 HIGH partials → "submission-ready" → delete the question sheet & history, pour into the venue template.

**Unfilled slots = yellow highlight (v2 spec)**: unfillable `[ ]` slots get yellow character shading in the DOCX, with the fulfillment question anchored as a comment. One glance shows what remains. Cleared when fulfilled. **Verify zero yellow in the submission version** (= leftover-gap detector). The old color-coded HTML is retired.

**Three principles**: ① no bare flags (every gap comes with a fulfillment question or a draft suggestion) ② always praise (what is done well is explicitly named) ③ no fabrication (results, numbers and citations are never invented — only what the user provides).

## 4.5 paper_template — the paper's full history in one file, 4 parts

**Part 1: Initial question sheet (Q1–Q12)** — working title, type, phase, venue (+ rationale), skeleton, language, method name, core claim, authors, deadline, material list, version
→ **Part 2: Literature Review** — generated & updated by literature-reviewer: bibliography + plain summary + full-text URL (flag = unverified source)
→ **Part 3: Paper draft area** — generated & extended slot by slot. Unfilled = flags + yellow highlight + comment
→ page break → **Part 4: Review history table (at the very end of the document)** — per version: praise / gap counts (vs. prior) / HIGH issues / fulfillment questions. Newest row on top

**History always goes at the back**: the review/update history sits at the very end of the document, preceded by a page break (the body stays readable; history is an appendix). The same rule applies to research-starter and research-proposer DOCX outputs (in a proposal it follows the Literature Review).

- Naming: `{YYYYMMDD}_#{VENUE}_{SHORT_TITLE}_@{FIRST_AUTHOR}_{LANG}_vX.docx` (e.g. `20260706_#EJC2027_TimeAxisSynth_@Takahashi_EN_v0.docx`)
- Version +1 per revision; date refreshed
- Parts 1, 2 and 4 stay until submission. In the camera-ready: transfer verified references into References, delete Part 2, then sweep Parts 1 & 4 and all yellow highlights (never transfer flagged sources)

## 4.6 literature-researcher / literature-reviewer — survey and verification, split

**literature-researcher (survey agent)**: playbook building at project start. ① top researcher list (5–10: names, affiliations, representative papers, Scholar/ORCID URLs) ② topic map (3–5 issue axes + your position) ③ world-line statement draft ④ initial playbook (candidate references, 70% post-2020, one-line notes). Called from research-starter Phase 04.

**literature-reviewer (verify agent)**
1. **Build the list**: confirm each work exists via web search and fix the bibliography. Compose to the 70% post-2020 rule and report the ratio
2. **Plain summaries**: summarize each work in a few lines a junior-high student can follow — what, how, what was found (rephrased, never copied)
3. **Verify full-text URLs**: resolve in priority order DOI → publisher → PubMed/PMC → arXiv (labeled preprint); check reachability. Unverifiable → **⚠️ source unknown, verify** + how to check

Call relations: paper-compiler → inserts Part 2 between the question sheet and the review history (page break) ; research-proposer → last section, after the FAQ ; also standalone ("build me a reference list").

**No fabrication (paramount)**: never generate unverified works, DOIs or URLs. If it cannot be confirmed, flag it. Flagged sources are never transferred into References until resolved.

## 4.7 audit — the 18-item pre-submission gate (paper-auditor)

| Category | Items |
|---|---|
| A Structure & prep (01–04) | 01 confirm original research article / 02 IMRAD vs. ILMRAD checked against the venue / 03 Methods chapter name matches / 04 Title: dense, searchable keywords |
| B Abstract (05–07) | 05 traces of a Working Abstract / 06 Final rewritten after the body / 07 Abstract + figures alone convey the skeleton |
| C References (08–11) | 08 overflow moved to Supplementary / 09 ~70% post-2020 (hako: N/A) / 10 arXiv labeled preprint (AI/ML OK) / 11 citation style matches the venue |
| D Chapter roles (12–17) | 12 I: gap & contributions explicit / 13 L: organized by issue / 14 M: reproducible detail / 15 R: no interpretation (hako: N/A) / 16 D: comparison, limits, implications / 17 Conclusion placement chosen deliberately |
| **E Closed-world logic (item 18, 7 sentences / A. Uraki, new in v2)** | Do the Abstract and early Introduction read, in order, as 1) humans do X → 2) unrealized in systems → 3) key challenge → 4) we propose? Has the world-view stayed closed? Out-of-order or partly missing = flag (with an insertion draft) |

Verdicts: ✅ ❌ ⚠️ N/A (evidence quotes required). Overall: accept / revise / major revision. 3+ fails or a HIGH issue proposes moving to review mode.

## 4.8 Suggest edits Word-natively — comment & tracked-change rules

- **Rewrites → tracked changes**: insertions and deletions recorded so the user can accept/reject each one in Word.
- **Reasons, questions, alternatives → comments**: anchored to the exact text. Slot-specific fulfillment questions appear in both the gap report and as comments.
- **Missing slots only → placeholder + flag + yellow**: a `[ ]` placeholder where the slot belongs, with the fulfillment question anchored as a comment.
- **Inline annotations forbidden**: never write notes like "[comment: …]" into the body. The history table holds summaries only.
- **Author-name rule (two identities)**: never "Claude" or a skill name.
  - Default: **`Yusuke Takahashi, PhD`** (`YT`) — every comment not listed below, and *all* tracked changes.
  - **`Asako Uraki, PhD`** (`AU`) — **only for findings rooted in the Uraki method**: ① the 7-sentence abstract / closed world (audit item 18) ② the 5-chapter hako template — fixed phrases, signature verbs, Abstract 1–6 (`paper-composer`) ③ cross-chapter consistency H-X01–18 (character-identical method name, notation, contribution-list matching) ④ the A/B decision protocol.
  - When in doubt, use the default. Tracked changes always carry the default identity (the Uraki identity is reserved for *comments*, i.e. findings).
  - Validate before output with `validate.py --original`.
- **Every edited location gets a comment (mandatory on each revision)**: for **every** passage changed from vX to vX+1 (newly generated, added, rewritten, deleted), leave a Word comment explaining *what* changed and *why*. Format: `[vX update] what changed / reason: the basis (slot ID, audit item, answer to a fulfillment question)`. Fulfilled slots are marked `flag → pass` and their yellow highlighting cleared at the same time. Changes such as a method rename get synchronization comments in every affected chapter. Comments from earlier versions are never deleted. This exists so you never receive a new version whose diff you cannot follow.
